Category A: simple questions about a single document

In Category A, you will create simple, direct questions based on a single SEC filing (10-K or 10-Q).

How to work on a Category A task:

  • Choose one filing (10-K or 10-Q) and read it carefully.
  • Identify a clear fact, and write a question that asks directly about that fact.
  • Write the answer exactly as it appears in the filing. Do not change the facts or numbers, do not round up or down, and do not exaggerate. Accuracy is essential.

Example (Good):
What was Coca‚ÄëCola‚Äôs total net operating revenue for the fiscal year ending December 31, 2024?

Rational:
It asks for a single, direct factual figure (total net operating revenue) that can be found in one filing only (the company‚Äôs 10-K).

2. Category B: complex questions about a single document

In Category B, you will create more challenging questions based on a single SEC filing (10-K or 10-Q). The answer requires combining multiple facts from different sections of the filing, making inferences, or computing numbers not readily available in the report.

How to work on a Category B task:

  • Choose one filing (10-K or 10-Q) and read it carefully.
  • Identify multiple related facts or numbers from different sections of the filing.
  • Write a question that requires using all these facts to determine the answer.

Example (Bad):
For The Coca-Cola Company's year ended December 31, 2024, what was the year-over-year percentage change in Net Operating Revenues?

Rational:
Although the question mentions YOY, it directly asks for the percentage increase, which is already explicitly stated in the Net Operating Revenues section of the filing.
The answer can be taken directly from a single line in the report.
The question asks for a stated figure rather than requiring computation, it is a Category A question, not Category B.

Example (Good):
For the fiscal year ended December 31, 2024, what percentage of Coca‚ÄëCola‚Äôs net operating revenues was consumed by its Cost of Goods Sold?

Rational:
This question requires linking two separate numbers from two sections in the filing, the Net Operating Revenues and the COGS, and performing a calculation to find a percentage that is not explicitly stated in the report.

3. Category C: hard questions using multiple reports

In Category C, you will create advanced questions that require using information from two or more SEC filings. These filings may belong to the same company across different periods or to multiple companies, often within the same sector.
The answer cannot be derived from a single document and requires cross-document analysis, comparison, or aggregation.

How to work on a Category C task:

  • Use 2 to 40 SEC filings (10-Ks and/or 10-Qs).
  • Extract related facts or numbers from each filing.
  • Write a question that cannot be answered using one report alone.

Example (Bad):
How did Coca-Cola‚Äôs net operating revenue change between 2023 and 2024?

Rational:
Although this question involves two years, both figures are available within the same 2024 filing.
The comparison can be completed using one document, so it does not meet the requirement of using multiple reports.

Example (Good):
Between Coca-Cola and PepsiCo, which company had a higher net operating revenue growth rate in 2024 compared to 2023?

Rational:
This question requires:
Using at least two different filings (one for Coca-Cola and one for PepsiCo).
Extracting revenue figures from each company‚Äôs filings.
Calculating year-over-year growth for both companies.
Comparing the results to reach a conclusion.
Because the answer depends on information from multiple reports and companies, and involves computation and comparison, this is a true Category C question.

‚úçÔ∏è How to Write a High-Quality Prompt

A good prompt is the foundation of a high-quality work. Below are the key characteristics every prompt must have.

1. Clear and Specific

The prompt should clearly state what information is being asked for without being vague, subjective, or ambiguous. Unclear prompts lead to incorrect or inconsistent answers.

Clear question = clear single answer from the filing. No room for subjectivity.

Example (Good):
What was the company‚Äôs total revenue for the fiscal year ended December 31, 2024?

Example (Bad):
Tell me about the company‚Äôs performance.

Rational:
This prompt is too broad and unclear. It does not specify: Which financial metric is required, Which time period to focus on. 20 possible answers exist.

2. Self-Contained and complete

The prompt should make sense on its own without needing additional context or external information. A self-contained prompt ensures anyone reading it can understand and answer it based solely on the filing.

Example (Good):
What were Apple Inc.‚Äôs total sales for the first quarter of 2025?

Example (Bad):
1- How did Tesla's research and development spending in 2023 compare to Ford's?

Rational:
It doesn‚Äôt specify how to compare: does it want the absolute difference in dollars, the percentage, or the ratio of spending to profit? The question is clear but incomplete.

2- What were the sales for Q1 2024?

Rational:
It doesn‚Äôt specify which company to refer to.

3. Time-Specific

The prompt must include clear, explicit dates or periods. Avoid vague words like ‚Äúcurrently,‚Äù ‚Äúnow,‚Äù or ‚Äúat that time.‚Äù

Prompts will be used later, so vague time references can become unclear or misleading.

Example (Good):
What was the company‚Äôs operating margin for the fiscal year ended December 31, 2024?

Example (Bad):
What is the company‚Äôs operating margin right now?

Rational:
‚ÄúRight now‚Äù is vague and can change over time

üö®Important Note

When writing the prompt, do not mention the filing type (10-K / 10-Q) or the filing date explicitly in the question.

Why this matters:

Our goal is to use this data (the prompts) to test the AI model's ability to identify and extract the correct information from the correct SEC filings. Including the filing type or filing date makes the task easier for the AI models. Our goal is to test whether the model can correctly locate and use the relevant report on its own, not to guide it directly to the answer.

Instead, make the question time-specific (for example, by referencing the fiscal year or period). This keeps the prompt natural and realistic while still being precise.

Example (Bad):

Why it‚Äôs bad:
The filing type and filing date are explicitly stated, which guides the AI directly to the correct report.

Example (Good):

Why it‚Äôs good:
The question is time-specific without revealing the filing type or filing date, making it more natural and more challenging.

4. Verifiable from the Filing Only

The prompt must be answerable entirely using information explicitly stated in the SEC filing. No assumptions, outside knowledge, or research should be needed. This ensures answers are factual, verifiable, and consistent, and avoids introducing errors or personal opinions.

Example (Good):
What specific risks did the company highlight in the Risk Factors section for the fiscal year ended December 31, 2024?

Example (Bad):
Do you think the CEO is doing a good job in allocating capital?

Rational:
Requires judgment, not directly stated in the filing. Cannot be answered factually from the report alone. Introduces subjectivity and inconsistency.

üîó Add Citations to your response

A citation is a short marker (such as a number or letter) added immediately after any fact, figure, statement, or information taken from the filing. It tells the reviewer exactly where that information appears in the document.

Citations are required for all factual information to ensure accuracy and verifiability.
Citation Rules:

  • Use numbers (1), (2), (3)‚Ä¶ OR letters (a), (b), (c)‚Ä¶
  • Put them in Parentheses () or Brackets [] , like (1) or [a].
  • ALWAYS place it immediately after the fact, e.g., ‚ÄúRevenue was $10M (1)‚Äù or ‚ÄúGrowth slowed [b]‚Äù.
  • Each citation marker must have a matching entry in the References section.

Why Citations Are Required

Citations allow reviewers to:

  • Verify the accuracy of the information.
  • Locate the exact place where the information appears in the filing.
  • Ensure consistency and reliability across responses.

Without citations, the information cannot be validated.

Examples:

Good: ‚ÄúNet sales rose 12% (1).‚Äù

Bad: ‚ÄúNet sales rose 12%.‚Äù

No citation is provided, so the information cannot be verified.

How to Add References

To write a reference, open the Table of Contents (usually the first 1-3 pages of the filing) and use the following format:

  • Part number (if shown): e.g., Part II
  • Item number (always): e.g., Item 7
  • Item name (always): e.g., MD&A
  • Page number (always): e.g., p.22
  • Extra (optional): e.g., para 2, Table 3

. If you are working on a Category C task, you must follow the format above and also include:

  • The type of the SEC form: e.g., 10-K
  • The filing date: e.g., (Nov. 3,2024)

Given this citation in a response: ‚ÄúNet sales rose 12% [b]‚Äù

. Example of good referencing for category A or B:

[b] Part II, Item 7, MD&A, p.22

. Example of good referencing for category C:

[b] Part II, Item 7, MD&A, p.22, 10-K, (Nov. 3,2024)

Using Multiple References for One Fact

Sometimes, a single fact or number in your response is based on more than one source.
This usually happens when the value is calculated, inferred, or derived using multiple parts of one filing or multiple filings.
In this case, you must cite all sources used to build that fact.

How to cite a fact based on multiple sources

If the fact in your response is derived from several sources, write it like this in the answer:

(Fact)[1][2][3]

Each citation marker refers to a different source used to reach that result.

How to write the References section

In the References section, you must:

  • Briefly state the specific information taken from that source.
  • Then write the full reference using the required format.
